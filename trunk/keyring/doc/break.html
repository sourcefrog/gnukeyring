GNU Keyring tries to keep your passwords secure through it's user interface and storage. One important feature of this is that passwords are encrypted when stored in the database and PC.

The EDE DES3 algorithm used to do this is well trusted, but a fundamental limit is the length of the master key. To achieve the full potential of the encryption algorithm, the master key would have to have 112 bits of entropy. Assuming a good mix of letters and other symbols, each character would contribute a bit over 6 bits of entropy. Therefore passwords over 18 characters are maximally secure, while anything under that is proportionally more vulnerable to a brute-force attack.

Since the password has to be entered each time the keyring is accessed I expect few people will use 18 character passwords. A more likely password of six randomly-selected printable characters gives about 40 bits of entropy, which is about a million million possible passwords. This is reasonably large, but not impossible to check by brute force on a modern PC.

There's no way that I know of to keep useful secrets on the handheld, and in any case keeping things there would prevent backing up the keyring and put it at risk of loss from hardware failure, battery exhaustion or pilot error.

Changing to a new encryption algorithm would not help the essential problem of predictable keys. One option would be to use something other than an alphanumeric password. Perhaps a freehand signature could be used to form a digital secret key?

Since it's probably possible to brute force the password, the most useful precaution is to only backup the keyring to a trusted PC. Some criteria for security to weigh against the confidentiality of the data in the keyring include:

  • The operating system and services on the machine should be up-to-date; and new fixes should be promptly applied as problems are found.
  • Only people you trust with the information in the keyring should have access to the machine.
  • The machine should be physically secure.
  • Backups of the machine should be made in a secure way and kept safe.
  • The machine should not be unduly network-accessible.
  • Whether the software on the machine can be trusted

I intend in the future to offer a utility to break keyrings on a PC as an aid to people who've lost their password. It's not terribly hard to write such a thing, so I can't assume me not writing it would give any additional protection. I'm not sure if I'll do this; please express your opinion here or mail me.
